Estimating interesting numbers using regular polygons with TileDrive

Regular polygons are easy to introduce but some of their properties are subtle and difficult to discuss in elementary school. One discussion that can be fruitful is about estimations of length of diagonals or heights. An easy-to-learn tool for creating polygonal art, TileDrive, can be used to quickly produce estimations. TileDrive's precise creation of polygons and its feature of removing overlapping polygons enable a visual way to compare and relate lengths associated with regular polygons.

Introduction

TileDrive (jimmorey.com/td/TileDrive.html) is a prototype designed to aid in creating polygons patterns with few prerequisites. Initially, patterns are intended to be created interactively adding edge connected polygons with the hope of eventually learning a simple language that essentially describes the same interactions. To the right, shows the main screen of the prototype: the left panel contains choices of simple actions, and a sequence of the actions below that were used so far in the pattern; the central panel holds an interactive canvas of the resulting pattern; the top panel contains a means of cutting and pasting; and finally, the optional bottom panel, advanced, contains a text-based means to automate the cutting and pasting (which is originally closed so as not to distract new users). The choices of simple actions can also be selected directly using keyboard input: 3-8 for polygons with that number of sides (in fact, 9012 will produce a 9gon, a 10gon, an 11gon, and a 12gon); rybogplit for colors red, yellow, blue, orange, green, purple, olive, pink, and transparent (in fact, k and a are used for black and gray); {} for branching. Without branching, a sequence of action produces a single path of polygons. With branching, a collection of paths of polyons can more easily make intricate patterns. For more details, check out TileDriveCoding

length exploration

TileDrive has no explicit coordinates or measuring interface. But tile paths can be used to estimate length and the removal of intersecting tiles can be exploited as a discrete means of indicating when lengths are too long. In particular, a row of squares can be used as a ruler and a tile path can be placed over top of the ruler to see how many squares are removed.

Things get a bit more difficult when looking into estimates for π since it does not involve a simple constructible length that comes from a single polygon. One way to estimate π, is to think about the circumference over the diameter which can be estimated with regular polygons. For overestimating, the polygon must be circumscribed (bigger than the circle) and the diameter underestimated. Similarly, for underestimating, the polygon must be inscribed (smaller than the circle) and the diameter overestimated. Below are a few examples with their numerical estimates.
