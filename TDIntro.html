Introduction to TileDrive: a spectrum of engagement from simple drawing to complex coding

a geometric coding environment

TileDrive is a simple environment for creating rich geometric patterns with few prerequisites. It offers a small, approachable set of instructions to master with the richness of the patterns coming from the subtleties of regular polygons (tiles). An understanding of distances, angles, and numbers is not required before creating with TileDrive. There are a few videos on YouTube that describe its basic use.


TileDriving

The key idea is to use tiles to drive through a pattern. Driving means going forward and turning right or left. Going forward occurs by placing tiles, and turning occurs discretely by switching the "forward" side to the right or the left of the current forward direction. Below are a few examples going forward with tiles with the ones to the right depicting a left and right turn. Note that the right and left turns related to square tiles are 90 degrees where as the bottom two related to hexgonal tiles are only 60 degree. As well, each turn occurs before the last tile is added.




The simplest way to drive is to use the on-screen buttons shown below. In addition to placing tiles and turning, there are 8 colors and a transparent option (used for creating disconnected patterns). An alternative and potentially faster way to drive is by using a keyboard. Each instruction is associated with a single key press. Rather than clicking on the images of the square and the arrows, the following sequence of keystrokes "4i444>4r" would produce a line of pink squares with a red one on the left of the last pink square. Notice that the last color is remembered (initially, red is the color before one is selected).

simple paths and interlacing sequences

Simple paths can be created by using one type of tile. The even-sided tiles by default get placed in a straight line, whereas the odd-sided tiles have a right-turning bias. To "drive" straight with odd-sided tiles, left turns must be performed every other tile to compensate for the right-turn bias. In a sense, "doing donuts" with odd-sided tiles is the default behavior, whereas even-sided tile donuts require more work. The red-colored tiles do not have turns, whereas the pink ones turn left.

  • straight driving:
    • even-sided: 4444, 6666, 8888
    • odd-sided: 33<33<33<33<, 55<55<55<, 77<77<77<
  • doing donuts:
    • even-sided: 4<4<4<4<, 6<6<6<6<6<6<, 8<8<8<8<8<8<8<8<
    • odd-sided: 333333, 5555555555, 77777777777777

Interlacing two sequences is a way to create more interesting patterns. The following examples show how to interlace two sequences. The first two examples don't require turns, relying on the right-turning bias to make donuts. The last two examples color the tiles with left turns pink, as with the straight driving and donuts examples above.

Rather than using colors to indicate turns, they can be used to indicate which sequence a tile came from or perhaps just asthetics.

branching and embellishing

Although driving tiles around can make a lot of interesting patterns, backing up or constructing elaborate paths to create what you want can be tedious and frustrating. Instead, by tapping on any existing tile in a pattern, a new path is created starting on that tile. For instance, to turn the yellow and blue pattern into a pendant, one could just click on one of the upper triangles and start a new path "driving" the blue chain of squares and triangles. With multiple additions to the same yellow and blue pattern, a fish could also be created. The orange and purple donut can be transformed into a face by adding a few extra paths. It can further be enhanced by using transparent tiles instead of orange ones. The final pink blue interlaced path can be embellished to become a pinwheel.

Branching and embellishing can be used to create fresh patterns that are not based on these donut patterns. The following patterns show some other examples that demonstrate branching paths. As a perk of using the keyboard shortcuts as opposed to using screen buttons is that there are a few additional instructions: the colours black and gray, and the tiles nonagons (9-gon), decagons (10-gon), and unadecagon (11-gon) with the keys k, a, 9, 0, and 1 respectively. The black tires —k, the gray elephant —a, the pink flower centre —9, the spider's cephalothorax (where the legs meet) —0 and the spider's abdomen —1.

repetition and banks

Where TileDrive shines is repetitive patterns. Although it is possible to create patterns one tile at a time, it is faster and less error-prone to reuse sequences of instructions. These sequences can be stored in a bank and used as a single instruction. The way banks are used are similar to cutting and pasting. There are ten banks available labeled A-J. They are loaded by cutting all the current instructions into a bank. They can be used by pasting them at the current tile.

    A repetitive pattern can be created by:
  • clicking on icons for square purple pentagon orange xA(cut A)
    • 4p5o xA
  • paste A ten times (by clicking on the picture above xA ) then turn right
    • vA vA vA vA vA vA vA vA vA vA >
  • cut to bank B and paste 5 fimes
    • xB vB vB vB vB vB

These interactions produce 205 instuctions or 80 resulting tiles. The benefit of using banks is not only that it speeds up creating patterns but it opens up a way of seeing and decribing regularity in patterns. It is probably critical to spend energy and time with TileDrive before using banks so that this benefit can be fully appreciated.

The construction above uses a single branch to create the pattern with some tiles being overwritten. Another way to construct this pattern would be to use branching.

    A branched version:
  • clicking on icons for square purple pentagon orange xA(cut A)
    • 4p5o xA
  • paste A six times and then square, purple
    • vA vA vA vA vA vA 4 p
  • click on the third pentagonal tile, turn right, paste A, and then square purple
    • > vA 4p
  • cut to bank B and then pentagon, red paste bank B click on the the red pentagon turn right and paste, repeat this time turning twice etc. until all five sides of the central red pentagon are completed

In a sense, the branching style of construction is nicer since there is no overwriting of tiles but it is more complicated to design. When examining the side bar that shows textual instructions of this pattern, there is more structure to this code than that the previous construction. These differences can also be explored and examined with animation using the on-screen @ button.

rainbow shortcut

Colours can make polygonal art much more appealing. To make it easier to manipuluate and play with colouring, some rainbow buttons(", ') were added: these go up and down a colour in the TileDrive rainbow (red, orange, yellow, green, olive, blue, purple, pink, then back to red). For example, if a tile is currently red, and colourUp (") is pressed then the colour becomes orange. Similarly, orange goes to yellow, yellow goes to olive, etc. until pink which goes back to red and starts again.

{555{>5}555}"> xA
5 vA vA vA vA vA

In this case, every chunk of 7 tiles(stored in bank A) is assigned a different colour with a colourUp command. Technically, each chunk of 7 inherits the central tile's colour then only the central tile gets an updated colour. The central blue colour tile resulted from the last colourUp (") from the olive colour. An additional bank A (vA) would replace the red chunk with a blue version of the chunk.

{555{>5}555}"> xA
5o vA vA vA vA vA
{555{>5}555}'> xA
5l vA vA vA vA vA

A different starting colour of orange of the first tile changes all of the colours of the pattern as can be seen in the pattern on the far right. As well, using colourDown (') changes the order of the colours in the pattern which can be this in the second pattern.

coding

Many interesting patterns can be produced using and reusing banks. One way to become more connected with the patterns is to become a poweruser and use the keyboard to speed up creating patterns. Another way to better understand these patterns is to use a built-in preprocessing language that incorporates the basic instructions with bank operations. This language can help focus attention on the describing and communicating the ways patterns relate to one another. As well, the language has a means of interlacing sequences and performing multiple pastes. The interface for this language is revealed by clicking on the "adv" button.

    Examples of simple programs
  • repeating multiple pastes with +#<bank>:
    • 5r5i, xA
      +4A
    • 3y3,8g, xA
      +8A
    • 4a xA
      4p{>+3A} xB
      {+3B}>xC
      4r+4C
  • interlacing sequences m<bank1><bank2>:
    • 5r5r5r xA
      5i,5i,5i, xB
      mAB
    • 8g< xA
      +8A xA
      3y3y< xB
      mAB
    • 4a{>2} xA
      4p{>+3A} xB
      {+3B}> xC
      4r+4C xD
      6o xE mDE

There are many details and subtleties in TileDrive coding that do not necessarily need to be explored by all students but can be left for enrichment or for the teachers to quickly create variations of patterns.
